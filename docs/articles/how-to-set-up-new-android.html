Step-by-Step Guide

How to Set Up a New Android Phone

By The Tech Brief Editorial Team Published: March 1, 2025 ~8 min read

Getting a new Android phone is exciting — but the setup process involves more decisions than it might appear. Done properly, you'll have your contacts, apps, messages, and settings transferred from your old device with minimal fuss. Done hastily, you may find yourself missing data or locked out of accounts. This guide covers every step, from the moment you power on to finishing with a fully configured daily driver.

Before You Turn on the New Phone: Prepare Your Old One

If you're switching from an existing Android phone, a few minutes of preparation on the old device makes the transfer dramatically smoother.

Back up the old phone

Go to Settings > Google > Backup on your old phone and tap Back up now. This saves your contacts, SMS, call history, app data, Wi-Fi passwords, and device settings to your Google account. For photos, open Google Photos and confirm backup is complete under your profile icon. Note your Google account email address — you'll need it during new phone setup.

Export WhatsApp and other app data

WhatsApp has its own backup system separate from Google Backup. In WhatsApp, go to Settings > Chats > Chat Backup and tap Back Up. This saves your chat history to Google Drive. Do the same for Telegram (Settings > Data and Storage > Backup) and any other messaging or productivity apps you use heavily.

Transfer your SIM or note your number for eSIM

Locate your SIM card in the old phone. Most modern Android phones use a nano-SIM. If your new phone supports eSIM, contact your carrier before setup — you may need a QR code or activation code to transfer your number to the new device without a physical SIM.

Step 1: Insert SIM and Power On

Insert your SIM card into the new phone (or activate eSIM later in setup). Press and hold the power button until the manufacturer's logo appears. The initial startup sequence takes 20–60 seconds.

Step 2: Complete the Setup Wizard

Android's setup wizard guides you through the critical configuration steps. Here's what each screen does and what to choose:

Language and accessibility

Select your preferred language. If you have accessibility needs (larger text, magnification, screen reader), this can also be configured here or adjusted later in Settings.

Connect to Wi-Fi

Connect to your home Wi-Fi network. The setup wizard and any subsequent app restore will use this connection heavily — connecting to Wi-Fi here prevents large mobile data charges and speeds up the process significantly.

Sign in with your Google account

Enter your Google account email and password. This is the account you backed up to earlier. If you have two-factor authentication enabled, you'll receive a verification prompt on a trusted device or your phone number. After signing in, you'll be asked whether to restore from a backup — choose the most recent backup from your old phone.

Data transfer from old phone

Many Android phones offer a cable-based transfer option at this stage, which is faster and more comprehensive than Google Backup for some data types. If both your old and new phones are present and connected via USB-C cable, use this option — it can transfer photos, videos, apps, settings, and even some app login states. If you prefer the wireless Google Backup restore, simply choose that option instead.

Step 3: Set Up Security

After the initial account setup, configure your lock screen and biometric authentication before anything else. This is important — if you skip it and the phone is lost or stolen before you set a PIN, the data on it is unprotected.

Set a PIN or password

Go to Settings > Security > Screen lock and choose PIN (recommended, 6+ digits) or Password. Avoid Pattern unlock — it's less secure and leaves smudge traces on the screen that can reveal the pattern.

Register your fingerprint

Go to Settings > Security > Fingerprint (or Biometrics on Samsung). Register at least two fingerprints — your dominant index finger and thumb. Some users register a finger from each hand for flexibility when the phone is held in different orientations.

Step 4: Install Updates

New phones often ship with a software version from several months earlier. Go to Settings > System > Software update and install any pending updates before you start customising the phone. This can take 10–30 minutes and may require a restart.

Step 5: Configure Google Services

Once you're signed in, review a few Google settings that have a meaningful impact on your experience and privacy:

  • Google Photos backup: Open Photos and confirm backup is enabled and set to the quality you prefer (Original Quality uses your Google storage quota; Storage Saver compresses photos)
  • Google Drive sync: If you use Drive for work files, sign in and confirm sync is active
  • Find My Device: Go to Settings > Google > Find My Device and enable it — this lets you locate, lock, or wipe the phone remotely if it's lost
  • Google Pay: Open the Google Wallet app and add your payment cards if you plan to use contactless payments

Step 6: Reinstall Your Essential Apps

If your restore from Google Backup is complete, most apps should already be installed. Check the app drawer and install any that are missing from the Play Store. For apps with their own login (banking, streaming, work tools), sign in with your credentials — these are not transferred via backup for security reasons.

Check whether your two-factor authentication app (Google Authenticator, Authy, or Microsoft Authenticator) transferred correctly. If it didn't, you'll need to re-scan QR codes or use recovery codes for each service. This is worth doing carefully and not skipping.

Step 7: Customise Your Home Screen and Notifications

Now that the functional setup is done, arrange your home screen. Long-press on any empty area to access the home screen editor. Move your most-used apps to the first page, remove anything you don't need, and consider creating folders for related apps to keep things organised.

For notifications, go to Settings > Notifications and review which apps have permission to send alerts. Disabling notifications for rarely used apps immediately reduces distractions and extends battery life slightly.
